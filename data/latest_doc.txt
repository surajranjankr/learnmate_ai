Different types of values
• Moral values – concern interpersonal behaviour,
e.g. being honest is desirable.
• Competence values – concern one’s own
valuation of one’s behaviour, e.g. behaving
imaginatively is desirable.
• Personal values – concern the ends, or terminal
states, that are desirable for the self, e.g. peace of
mind.
• Social values – concern the ends that one would
desire for society, e.g. world peace is desirable.

[Page 8]
Instrumental and Terminal Values
• Instrumental Values: how a person should live
and behave.
• Terminal Values: the ends or purposes that we
should be striving for.

Philosophical Views on Values
• Traditionalist: individual values reflect larger
social/institutional values and they are unquestionable.
• Modernist: values can be clearly stated and defined
through formal and rational debate.
• Neo-traditionalist: organisations and societies can have
unified values and these values act as a glue which
holds them together.
• The postmodernist: nothing in the social and
intellectual world as tangible or fixed.
• The pragmatist: skeptic about the possibility of an
objective truth and of a fixed hierarchy of values.

Utilitarianism
• To bring about greatest possible happiness to
all those who are concerned with the actions
(John Stuart Mill, 1806-1873).
• Right action for a particular occasion is the
one that produces more utility than any other
possible action.

[Page 20]
The Hedonic Calculus
• REMOTENESS – how near it is
• PURITY – how free from pain it is
• RICHNESS – to what extent it will lead to other
pleasures
• INTENSITY – how powerful it is
• CERTAINTY – how likely it is to result
• EXTENT – how many people it affects
• DURATION – how long it lasts
